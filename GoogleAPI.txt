Here are the properties for each relation on the positive integers:

## **(i)  Relation defined by:**

**x R y  ⇔  x·y is a perfect square**

### **Reflexive?**

Yes.
For any positive integer (x),
[
x \cdot x = x^2,
]
which is always a square. ✔️

### **Symmetric?**

Yes.
If (x \cdot y) is a square, then (y \cdot x) is the same number. ✔️

### **Antisymmetric?**

No.
Example: (1 \cdot 4 = 4) is a square, so (1R4) and (4R1), but (1 \ne 4). ❌

### **Transitive?**

Yes.
If
[
xy = a^2 \quad \text{and} \quad yz = b^2,
]
then for every prime (p),

* (v_p(x)+v_p(y)) is even,
* (v_p(y)+v_p(z)) is even,

Adding them gives
[
v_p(x)+2v_p(y)+v_p(z) \text{ is even} \implies v_p(x)+v_p(z) \text{ is even},
]
so (xz) is a perfect square. ✔️

### **Conclusion for (i):**

**Reflexive, Symmetric, Transitive (but not Antisymmetric).**
It **is** an equivalence relation.

## **(ii) Relation defined by:**

**x R y ⇔ x + y = 10**

### **Reflexive?**

No.
Reflexive would require (x + x = 10) for *all* (x), but only (x = 5) satisfies this. ❌

### **Symmetric?**

Yes.
(x + y = 10) implies (y + x = 10). ✔️

### **Antisymmetric?**

No.
Example: (3 + 7 = 10), so (3R7) and (7R3), but (3 \ne 7). ❌

### **Transitive?**

No.
Example:

* (3R7) because (3+7=10)
* (7R3) because (7+3=10)
  but (3R3) is false since (3+3 \ne 10). ❌

### **Conclusion for (ii):**

**Only Symmetric.**
